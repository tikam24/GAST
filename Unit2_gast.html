Atomic Theory — Acharya Kanada

Atomic theory was formulated over 2,600 years ago — more than 2,500 years before John Dalton — by Indian sage and philosopher Acharya Kanada (real name: Kashyap). He proposed his ideas in the Sanskrit text Vaisesika Sutra and founded the Vaisheshika School of Philosophy.

Key Facts at a Glance
  • Kanada lived between the 6th and 2nd centuries BCE (~600–800 BCE)
  • Called atoms "Anu" (atom) or "Paramanu" (ultimate particle)
  • "Kan" in Sanskrit means the smallest particle — hence the name Kanada
  • Atoms are indestructible, invisible to naked eye, and eternal
  • Atoms of the same substance combine to form dvyanuka (diatomic) and tryanuka (triatomic) molecules
  • Atoms can combine under heat to produce chemical changes
  • Kanada's theory was likely independent of the ancient Greek atomic theory

The Seven Predicables (Padarthas)

Kanada classified all knowable reality into six categories (padartha) — a 7th was added by Vedic scholars in the 10th century:

Vaisheshika Six (+ One) Predicables
  • Dravya (Substance) — Earth, Water, Fire, Air, Ether, Time, Direction, Soul, Mind
  • Guna (Quality) — Color, Taste, Smell, Touch, Number, Sound, etc.
  • Karma (Action) — Upward, Downward, Contraction, Expansion, Motion
  • Samanya (Generality) — Superior and Inferior categories
  • Vishesha (Uniqueness) — Particularity of each thing
  • Samavaya (Inherence) — Integration as part of the whole
  • Abhava (Non-existence) — Added ~10th century CE: Antecedent, Subsequent, Mutual, Absolute
Kanada's Profound Definitions
  • Life = an organized form of atoms and molecules
  • Death = an unorganized form of those atoms and molecules

History of Electricity in Ancient India

Evidence for knowledge of electricity in India dates back to around 10,000–8,000 BCE. Electricity is explicitly cited in the 5th Mandal of the Rigveda. Sage Agastya described battery construction in the Agastya Samhita (~8000 BCE).

Three Sources of Light and Energy (Rigveda)
  • 1. The Sun — source of daytime light and energy
  • 2. Electricity — used for illumination and comfort
  • 3. Fire inside the Earth — geothermal energy

The Agastya Samhita Battery (~8000 BCE)

Earthen Pot + Copper Plate + Mercury + Zinc Powder + Wet Saw Dust → Electric Current (called "Mitra-Varuna Samjnita") Compound of Rayi (Positive) + Prana (Negative) = Electricity

Electricity in the Mahabharata
  • Vedavyasa described atmospheric electricity generation in the Mahabharata (Vanaparva 133.26.7)
  • Rayi = Positive charge; Prana = Negative charge
  • Friction between clouds generates electricity; thunder falls due to gravity
  • The Ashtavakra dialogue describes the scientific process of atmospheric electricity — centuries before Benjamin Franklin (1750s)

Laws of Motion — Kanada vs Newton

Rishi Kanada's Vaisheshika Sutra (600 BCE) states three fundamental sutras that mirror Newton's three laws of motion — published in 1687 AD. Kanada identified five types of motion: Upward, Downward, Release of tensile stress, Shearing stress, and General motion.

First Sutra — Law of Inertia
वेगः निमित्तविशेषात कर्मणो जायते
Vegah Nimitta Visheshat Karmano Jayate
"Change of motion is due to impressed force."
Second Sutra — Law of Force & Acceleration
वेगः निमित्तापेक्षात कर्मणो जायते नियतदिक् क्रियाप्रबन्धहेतु
Vegah Nimitta Pekshat Karmano Jayate Niyatdik Kriya Prabandha Hetu
"Change of motion is proportional to the impressed force and is in the direction of the force."
Third Sutra — Law of Action-Reaction
वेगः संयोगविशेषविरोधी
Vegah Sanyog Vishesh Virodhi
"Action and reaction are equal and opposite."

05

Theory of Gravitation — Bhaskaracharya

Bhaskaracharya (Bhaskara II, 12th century CE) described a comprehensive theory of gravity approximately 1,200 years before Newton. He stated its principles in Surya Siddhanta and Siddhanta Shiromani.

Bhaskaracharya's Gravitational Principles
  • Gravity is a universal force — every mass attracts every other mass
  • The greater the mass, the greater the gravitational force
  • Objects stay on Earth due to Gurutvakarshan Shakti (Earth's gravitational pull)
  • Tidal waves are caused by the Moon's gravitational pull
  • The Sun's gravity keeps all planets in their orbits
  • There is mutual attraction between all planets
  • Earth's shape is spherical, not flat (explained via the curved circle analogy)
Bhaskaracharya's Calculation of Earth's Orbital Period: 365.2588 days (his value) 365.2563 days (modern accepted value) Difference: only 3.5 minutes!
Key Source Texts
  • Surya Siddhanta — First principle of gravity; Earth's spherical shape; orbital mechanics
  • Siddhanta Shiromani — Universal gravitation; orbital stability of planets and stars
  • Lilavati — Gurutvakarshan Shakti (gravitational force); mutual planetary attraction
  • Karanakuthuhala — Astronomical calculations; still used for precise calendars today

Key Indian Scientists in Physics

Acharya Kanada (Kashyap)
~600–800 BCE · Prabhas Kshetra, near Dwaraka, Gujarat · Philosophy & Physics

Founder of the Vaisheshika School. Originally named Kashyap, he was renamed Kanada ("particle collector") after picking up grains of rice at Prayaga, reasoning that even a single grain has cosmic importance — just as a single atom is fundamental to all matter.

  • First to propose atomic theory; named atoms "Anu" and "Paramanu"
  • Identified diatomic (dvyanuka) and triatomic (tryanuka) molecules
  • Proposed Gurutva (gravity) as cause of falling objects
  • Formulated three laws of motion analogous to Newton's laws
  • Classified reality into 7 aspects; matter (Dravyam) into 9 sub-aspects
  • Defined life as organized atomic form; death as disorganized form
Bhaskaracharya (Bhaskara II)
1114–1185 CE · Born near Bijapur, Karnataka · Mathematics, Physics, Astronomy

One of the greatest mathematician-astronomers of the medieval era. Headed the astronomical observatory at Ujjain. Named his first famous work Lilavati after his daughter.

  • First stated the theory of gravity — ~1,200 years before Newton
  • Calculated Earth's orbital period: 365.2588 days (modern: 365.2563 — error of just 3.5 min)
  • Pioneer of differential calculus — conceived differential coefficient theorem
  • First to state: a finite number divided by zero = infinity
  • Described Earth's spherical shape using mathematical circle analogy
  • Main work: Siddhanta Shiromani (Lilavati, Bijaganita, Grahaganita, Goladhaya)
Aryabhatta
476–550 CE · Pataliputra (Bihar) or Kerala · Mathematics & Astronomy

The earliest known mathematician-astronomer of India. Wrote the Aryabhatiya at age 23 (499 CE). Considered a genius whose theories continue to surprise modern mathematicians.

  • Invented 'zero' and the place-value decimal system
  • Knew Earth rotates on its axis and revolves around the Sun
  • Calculated circumference of Earth: 39,968 km (modern: 40,072 km)
  • Explained solar and lunar eclipses scientifically
  • Established 365 days as the exact length of the year
  • India's first satellite was named "Aryabhatta" in his honour
Sir C.V. Raman
7 Nov 1888 – 21 Nov 1970 · Tiruchirappalli, Tamil Nadu · Optics, Light Physics

First Indian scientist to receive the Nobel Prize. Sacrificed a lucrative government career for science. Declined a Cambridge professorship to serve India.

  • Discovered the Raman Effect on 28 February 1928 — scattering of light by molecules
  • Nobel Prize in Physics, 1930 — first Indian Nobel laureate in science
  • 28 February observed as National Science Day in India
  • Member of Royal Society (1924); Knighthood (1929)
  • Director, Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore (1933)
  • Students included Dr Homi Bhabha and Dr Vikram Sarabhai
Satyendra Nath Bose
1 Jan 1894 – 4 Feb 1974 · Kolkata · Quantum Physics

Famous for the Bose-Einstein Theory and the class of particles named Bosons after him. His work is connected to the discovery of the Higgs Boson ("God Particle").

  • Published "Max Planck's Law and Light Quantum Hypothesis" (1924)
  • Einstein translated his paper to German himself — named Bose-Einstein Theory
  • Appointed professor at Dhaka University on Einstein's recommendation
  • Fellow of the Royal Society, London (1958)
  • Awarded Padma Bhushan by Government of India
Homi Jahangir Bhabha
30 Oct 1909 – Jan 1966 · Mumbai · Nuclear Physics, Cosmic Radiation

The main architect of India's Atomic Energy Programme. Died in a plane crash near Mont Blanc while heading to a meeting of the International Atomic Energy Agency.

  • Brilliant research on cosmic radiation (1930–39); Fellow of Royal Society at age 31
  • Founded Tata Institute of Fundamental Research (TIFR) — inaugurated December 1945
  • Led India's atomic energy programme for 22 years (1944–1966)
  • Worked under C.V. Raman at IISc Bangalore
Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar
19 Oct 1910 – 21 Aug 1995 · Lahore · Astrophysics, Stellar Evolution

Nephew of C.V. Raman. PhD at Cambridge, 1933. Spent his career at the University of Chicago. Nobel Prize in Physics, 1983.

  • Discovered the Chandrasekhar Limit — max mass (~1.44 solar masses) for a white dwarf star
  • His work led to the discovery of neutron stars and black holes
  • Three possible final stages of a star: white dwarf, neutron star, black hole
  • Nobel Prize in Physics, 1983 (jointly with W.A. Fowler)
Vikram Ambalal Sarabhai
12 Aug 1919 – 31 Dec 1971 · Ahmedabad · Space Science, Cosmic Rays

"Father of the Indian Space Programme." Studied under C.V. Raman at IISc. PhD from Cambridge. Died in his sleep at age 52.

  • Founded ISRO (Indian Space Research Organisation)
  • Established India's first rocket launching station, TERLS in Thumba (21 November 1963)
  • Established Physical Research Laboratory, Ahmedabad (1947)
  • Pioneer researcher in solar physics and cosmic rays
  • Awarded Shanti Swarup Bhatnagar Medal (1962) and Padma Bhushan (1966)
Meghnad Saha
6 Oct 1893 – 16 Feb 1956 · Near Dhaka, Bangladesh · Astrophysics, Nuclear Physics

Rose from extreme poverty to become one of India's greatest astrophysicists. Instrumental in building India's scientific institutions.

  • Developed the Saha Equation — describes ionization conditions in stars
  • Fellow of the Royal Society, London (1927)
  • Built India's first cyclotron; introduced nuclear physics in university curriculum
  • Founded Saha Institute of Nuclear Physics (Kolkata, 1943)
  • Chaired India's Calendar Reform Committee (1952) — created the unified national calendar
  • Founded Science and Culture journal; elected Member of Parliament

Quick Self-Test

Click each question to reveal the answer. Try to recall before checking!

Q1Who first proposed atomic theory, and approximately when?Reveal ▾
Acharya Kanada (~600–800 BCE), in his Vaisesika Sutras — over 2,500 years before John Dalton.
Q2What are dvyanuka and tryanuka?Reveal ▾
Dvyanuka = diatomic molecule (two atoms of the same substance combined). Tryanuka = triatomic molecule (three atoms combined).
Q3Name the six types of electricity classified in ancient India.Reveal ▾
TADITA (rubbing silk), SAUDAMINI (rubbing gems), VIDYUT (thunder), SATAKUMBHI (hundred cells), HRDANI (stored/portable), ASANI (magnetic rods).
Q4What are "Rayi" and "Prana" in ancient physics?Reveal ▾
Rayi = Positive electric charge. Prana = Negative electric charge. Their compound state is collectively called Electricity.
Q5State Kanada's Third Sutra and its Newton equivalent.Reveal ▾
"Vegah Sanyog Vishesh Virodhi" — Action and reaction are equal and opposite. This is equivalent to Newton's Third Law of Motion.
Q6How did Bhaskaracharya describe the Earth's shape?Reveal ▾
He said Earth is spherical, not flat. Analogy: if you draw a very big circle and look at one-fourth of its circumference, it appears as a straight line — but it is actually circular. Similarly, Earth is spherical.
Q7What was Bhaskaracharya's calculation of Earth's orbital period, and how close is it?Reveal ▾
365.2588 days. The modern accepted measurement is 365.2563 days — a difference of only 3.5 minutes.
Q8What does the Rigveda verse "Seven horses draw the chariot of the sun" describe scientifically?Reveal ▾
The seven horses represent the seven colors of the visible light spectrum (VIBGYOR). The snake symbolizes the curved/wave path of light propagation.
Q9What speed of light is calculated from the Rigveda?Reveal ▾
~185,016 miles/second (2202 Yojanas per half-Nimisha). Modern value is 186,282.397 miles/second — the Vedic calculation is about 99.3% accurate.
Q10What is the Raman Effect and when was it discovered?Reveal ▾
The scattering of light by molecules (inelastic scattering). Discovered by C.V. Raman on 28 February 1928. This day is observed as National Science Day in India.
Q11What is the Chandrasekhar Limit and what did it lead to?Reveal ▾
The maximum mass (~1.44 solar masses) a white dwarf star can have. Stars exceeding this limit collapse further — leading to the discovery of neutron stars and black holes.
Q12Who founded ISRO and is called "Father of the Indian Space Programme"?Reveal ▾
Dr Vikram Sarabhai. He also established TERLS (India's first rocket launching station, 21 November 1963) and the Physical Research Laboratory, Ahmedabad.
Q13What is the Saha Equation and who developed it?Reveal ▾
Developed by Meghnad Saha. It describes the chemical and physical conditions (ionization states) in stellar atmospheres, fundamental to astrophysics.
Q14Name the four-fold process of speech production in Vedic science, with chakra locations.Reveal ▾
Para (Muladhara/coccygeal), Pashyanti (Manipuraka/navel), Madhyama (Anahata/heart), Vaikhari (Vishuddhi/throat — audible speech).
Q15Why is the element Akasa significant in Indian physics?Reveal ▾
Akasa corresponds to the nuclear state of matter — a state not recognized by ancient Greek or Roman philosophers. It represents the fifth element, unique to Indian philosophy.
Q16What connected Satyendra Nath Bose to Albert Einstein?Reveal ▾
Bose sent his 1924 paper "Max Planck's Law and Light Quantum Hypothesis" to Einstein, who was so impressed he translated it into German himself and sent it for publication. The theory was named Bose-Einstein Theory.
Q17What are the nine Rasas (emotional moods) in Indian classical music?Reveal ▾
Shringaara (amorous), Veera (heroic), Karuna (pathetic), Haasya (comic), Adbhua (marvelous), Raudra (ferocious), Bhayanaka (frightening), Bheebhatsa (odious), Shaanta (peaceful).
Q18Why was Kanada's formulation of the laws of motion qualitative rather than quantitative?Reveal ▾
Because digits were not yet invented in Kanada's time (zero was invented by Aryabhata; infinity by Bhaskaracharya, both later). Without a proper number system, Kanada could only state the laws in qualitative verbal/sutra form.
