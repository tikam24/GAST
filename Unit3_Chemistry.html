01

Overview — Chemistry in Ancient India

Chemistry as a formal discipline took shape in 18th-century Europe, after centuries of alchemical tradition partly borrowed from Arabs. However, India had a vastly older tradition of chemical knowledge stretching back thousands of years, rooted in both practical application and deep philosophical inquiry.

Key Overview Points
  • Modern chemistry is a relatively young European discipline (18th century)
  • European alchemy aimed at turning base metals into gold and finding the elixir of life
  • India had a far older, richer tradition — the Rasashastra (science of mercury/essence)
  • Indian chemical knowledge was deeply intertwined with medicine, philosophy, and spirituality
  • Evidence of advanced chemistry found in Indus Valley Civilization (~2500–1500 BCE)
  • India's chemical traditions fused elaborate techniques with an intentional spiritual component
02

Objectives of Indian Chemistry

In ancient India, chemistry was largely an offshoot of alchemy. The alchemical tradition had two primary objectives — both aimed at extraordinary transformations.

Objective 1
DEHAVEDHA / DEHA SIDDHI
Transformation of the Human Body

Making the body resistant to all disease through the Elixir of Life (Amrita), resulting in longevity and ultimately the attainment of salvation while still alive — called Jivan Mukti.
Objective 2
LOHAVEDHA / LAUHA SIDDHI
Transformation of Base Metals into Gold

Converting base metals (lead, tin, copper) into gold with the help of mercury, through the Rasavidya tradition. Mercury called Svarnakara — "maker of gold".

04

Atomism in Vaisesika — Chemical Perspective

The Vaisesika philosophical system, founded by Kanada (literally "eater of particles"), presented one of the earliest atomic theories in history and provided the philosophical foundation for Rasashastra.

Core Atomic Concepts
  • "Kanada" means eater of particles — 'kana' = smallest particle in Sanskrit
  • All matter consists of indivisible units called Anu or Paramanu (atoms)
  • Atoms are eternal, indestructible, spherical, suprasensible, and in motion at primordial state
  • Atoms form pairs (dvyanuka) and triplets (tryanuka), among other combinations
  • Unseen forces cause interactions between atoms — analogous to modern chemical bonding

Nine Types of Substance (Dravya)

1. Prithvi (Earth)
Solid state — earth, stone, metals
2. Ap (Water)
Liquid state — fluids, solutions
3. Tejas (Fire)
Plasma/energy state — fire, light
4. Vayu (Wind)
Gaseous state — air, vapours
5. Akasa (Ether) ★
Space/nuclear state — unique to Indian philosophy; absent from Greek thought
6. Kala (Time)
Temporal dimension
7. Dik (Direction)
Spatial dimension
8. Manas (Mind)
The perceiving faculty
9. Atman (Spirit)
The knowing self

06

The Classical Age — Age of Alchemy

The Classical Age saw the flowering of Indian alchemy as a systematic discipline, known by several names.

Rasashastra
Science of Mercury/Essence — the most common name for Indian alchemy
Rasavidya
Knowledge of Rasa (mercury/essence)
Dhutavada
Doctrine of metals — focused on metallurgical transformation
Rasa
Central concept — means essence, taste, sap, juice, or mercury depending on context
Cinnabar (HgS) — The Key Mineral
  • Cinnabar = mercuric sulphide (HgS) — the primary ore of mercury
  • Beautiful orange-red to dark red mineral — prized for both color and mercury content
  • Ground into a red pigment called Vermilion in ancient times
  • Mercury extracted from cinnabar by roasting (a form of thermal chemical reduction)
07

Nagarjuna — The Master of Indian Alchemy

Nagarjuna is celebrated as the greatest figure in Indian alchemy (Rasashastra). He developed systematic mercury-based chemical preparations that were believed to confer both medicinal and occult powers.

The 18 Samskara — Mercury Purification Process

Despite mercury compounds being poisonous, ancient alchemists purified cinnabar through 18 complex processes:

Step 1–6
Rubbing with various medicinally efficacious plant juices and extracts
Step 7–12
Incorporation of sulphur, mica, and alkaline substances
Step 13–16
Multiple stages of purification, heating, and trituration
Step 17–18
Treatment with specific herbal combinations; final testing
Outcome
Mercury compound declared fit for consumption; believed to lead to body rejuvenation
Powers Attributed to Purified Mercury
  • Conferring longevity and resistance to disease
  • Occult powers: including invisibility and levitation
  • Ultimately: immortality and Jivan Mukti (liberation while alive)
08

Mercury (Rasaraja) and Sulphur — The Great Pair

In the Tantric alchemical tradition, mercury and sulphur were cosmic principles — their interaction was seen as a microcosm of the universal creative process. Most alchemical texts were written as dialogues between Shiva (mercury) and Shakti (sulphur).

MERCURY (Parada)
Rasaraja — King of Metals
  • Represents the MALE PRINCIPLE — Shiva
  • Liquid metal — unique physical property
  • Forms amalgams with other metals
  • Central to Deha Siddhi and Lauha Siddhi
  • Chemical symbol: Hg
SULPHUR (Gandhaka)
The Counterpart
  • Represents the FEMALE PRINCIPLE — Shakti
  • Yellow solid with characteristic smell
  • Combines with mercury to form cinnabar (HgS)
  • Sanskrit: Gandhaka = "smelly substance"
  • Chemical symbol: S
Mercury (Shiva) + Sulphur (Shakti) → Cinnabar (HgS) Cinnabar + 18 Samskara → Rasaraja (purified mercury) Rasaraja → Medicinal preparation / Alchemical agent
09

Transmutation of Base Metals

Transforming base metals (lead, tin, copper) into gold was a central pursuit. Mercury — called Svarnakara ("maker of gold") — played the key role. The process combined five physical operations with strict spiritual requirements.

Five Operations of Transmutation

OperationSanskritDescription
i. SmearingLepanaCoating the base metal with mercury paste or alchemical preparations
ii. ThrowingKsepaAdding reactive substances to the mixture
iii. PouringSecanaIntroducing liquids — plant juices, acids, or mercury compounds
iv. FumigatingDhupanaExposing metals to vapours of sulphur or other volatile substances
v. ImpactGhanaHammering or mechanical working of the treated metal
Spiritual Requirements for the Rasavadin (Alchemist)
  • Honesty and self-control
  • Sincerity of purpose
  • Devotion to God
  • Obedience to the Guru
  • Faith in Rasavidya — transmutation was NOT a purely mechanical process

The Laboratory — Rasashala

The ancient Indian alchemical laboratory was called the Rasashala ("hall of mercury/essence"). Its layout was carefully prescribed — combining practical functionality with ritual significance.

Four Doors
Facing the four cardinal directions
East
Location of Rasalinga — esoteric symbol representing mercury/Shiva
Southeast
Location of furnaces
Northwest
Location of instruments (Yantras)
Rasamantapa
Sacred worship pavilion adjacent to the Rasashala for worshipping Rasalinga
11

Apparatus — The Yantras

Ancient Indian alchemical laboratories were equipped with sophisticated apparatus called Yantras. Basic equipment included mortars, pestles, bellows, sieves, pans, tongs, and vessels. Seven specialized Yantras are described below.

MUSA YANTRA
Crucible
Use
Heating and melting metals; containing high-temperature reactions
Material
White clay, or earth of an anthill mixed with rice husk, iron dust, chalk, etc.
Note
Came in various shapes and sizes depending on application — equivalent to the modern laboratory crucible
KOSHTI YANTRA
Metal Essence Extractor
Use
Extraction of 'essences' of metals
Design
Two rimmed vessels; fire urged from above; side blower; vessels filled with charcoal and metal
Process
Heat and reducing atmosphere extract the desired metallic essence from the material
SWEDANI YANTRA
Steaming / Sweating Apparatus
Use
Softening substances; Swarasa extraction; Parada Sanskar; Rasa Dravya Shodhan (purification)
Etymology
'Swedana' = sweat in Sanskrit. After heating, material looks covered in droplets — as if it sweated
Design
Mud pot with water, wrapped in cloth, covered with a Sarava (lid), fire below. Steam generated purifies the substance
DOLA YANTRA
Swinging Purification Vessel
Use
Rasa Dravya Shodhan; Parada Shodhan (mercury purification); Pottali preparation
Design
A Pottali (cloth bundle) with substance is suspended by a thread from a wooden rod and swung in liquid in a pot over fire
Process
Swinging mechanical action combined with liquid medium achieves thorough purification
PATANA YANTRA
Sublimation / Distillation Apparatus
Use
Sublimation or distillation; distillation of Parada (mercury); extraction of herbal volatile oils
3 Types
Urdhva Patana (upward) · Adho Patana (downward) · Tiryak Patana (sideways)
Tiryak Type
Uses a still, condenser, and receiver — closely resembles a modern distillation apparatus
ADHANA YANTRA
Descending Vapour Apparatus
Use
Combining mercury vapours with other substances; vapour-phase reactions
Design
Mercury paste coated at the bottom of upper vessel; vapours descend into lower vessel; combine with substances kept there
Modern Analog
Analogous to chemical vapor deposition (CVD) — a sophisticated early form of chemical synthesis
DHUPA YANTRA
Fumigation Apparatus
Use
Fumigation of gold leaves or silver foils with fumes of sulphur or other substances
Design
Multi-chamber vessel — gold/silver in upper chamber, fumigant (sulphur) in lower chamber. Fumes rise and react with the metal surface
Modern Analog
Analogous to surface treatment and metal fumigation in modern metallurgy
Yantras Quick Reference
  • Musa Yantra — Crucible for heating/melting metals
  • Koshti Yantra — Two-vessel system for extracting metal essences
  • Swedani Yantra — Steam apparatus for softening and purification
  • Dola Yantra — Swinging vessel for mercury/rasa purification
  • Patana Yantra — Upward/downward/sideways distillation or sublimation
  • Adhana Yantra — Descending vapour reactor for combination reactions
  • Dhupa Yantra — Fumigation apparatus for metal surface treatment

Q1What are the two main objectives of Indian alchemy?Reveal ▾
1. Dehavedha/Deha Siddhi — transformation of the human body for longevity and Jivan Mukti (liberation while alive). 2. Lohavedha/Lauha Siddhi — transformation of base metals into gold using mercury.
Q2What does 'Rasa' mean in Indian alchemy?Reveal ▾
'Rasa' has many meanings (essence, taste, sap, juice) but in alchemy specifically refers to mercury — seen as the most important element. Mercury is the 'king of rasa' — Rasaraja.
Q3What is Cinnabar and what is its chemical formula?Reveal ▾
Cinnabar is mercuric sulphide (HgS). It is a beautiful orange-red mineral, the primary ore of mercury, and was ground into the red pigment Vermilion in ancient times.
Q4Name the three Sanskrit names for Indian alchemy.Reveal ▾
Rasashastra (science of mercury), Rasavidya (knowledge of rasa), and Dhutavada (doctrine of metals).
Q5What does 'Kanada' literally mean?Reveal ▾
Kanada literally means 'eater of particles' — 'kana' means the smallest particle in Sanskrit. It reflects his obsession with understanding the smallest units of matter (atoms).
Q6What are the five operations in transmutation of base metals?Reveal ▾
i. Smearing (Lepana), ii. Throwing (Ksepa), iii. Pouring (Secana), iv. Fumigating (Dhupana), v. Impact (Ghana).
Q7What is the Rasashala and describe its directional layout.Reveal ▾
The Rasashala is the ancient Indian alchemical laboratory. Layout: four doors (cardinal directions), Rasalinga (esoteric symbol) in the east, furnaces in the southeast, instruments in the northwest, and the Rasamantapa (worship pavilion) adjacent to it.
Q8How were mercury and sulphur symbolically represented?Reveal ▾
Mercury = MALE PRINCIPLE (Shiva); Sulphur = FEMALE PRINCIPLE (Shakti). Most alchemical texts were written as dialogues between Shiva and Shakti.
Q9How many samskara (processes) did cinnabar undergo? What did they include?Reveal ▾
18 complex samskara, including: rubbing with medicinally efficacious plant juices and extracts, incorporation of sulphur, mica, and alkaline substances, multiple stages of heating and trituration.
Q10What is a Musa Yantra and what is it made of?Reveal ▾
A crucible. Made of white clay or of the earth of an anthill mixed with rice husk, iron dust, chalk, etc. Came in various shapes and sizes depending on application.
Q11What are the three orientations of the Patana Yantra?Reveal ▾
Urdhva Patana (upward distillation), Adho Patana (downward distillation), and Tiryak Patana (sideways distillation).
Q12What is the Swedani Yantra and why is it named so?Reveal ▾
'Swedana' means sweat in Sanskrit. After the heating process, the material looks covered in water droplets as if it sweated. Used for softening substances, Swarasa extraction, Parada Sanskar, and Rasa Dravya Shodhan.
Q13What spiritual qualities were required of a Rasavadin (alchemist)?Reveal ▾
Honesty, self-control, sincerity of purpose, devotion to God, obedience to the guru, and faith in Rasavidya. Transmutation was not regarded as a purely mechanical process.
Q14What does the Kautilya Arthashastra contain from a chemistry perspective?Reveal ▾
Characteristics of precious stones (pearl, ruby, beryl); details of fermented juices (sugarcane, jaggery, honey, jambu, jackfruit, mango); and oil extraction techniques.
Q15What modern chemistry technique is most similar to the Adhana Yantra?Reveal ▾
Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD). Mercury paste on the upper vessel generated vapours that descended into the lower vessel to combine with substances there — an early form of vapor-phase chemical synthesis.
