Overview & Golden Age

Indian mathematics has a history spanning over 5,000 years, predating European advances and influencing civilizations across the globe — from China and Southeast Asia to the Middle East and Europe.

Major Contributions at a Glance
  • Concept of Zero — invented and formalized in India
  • Decimal place-value system — the global standard today
  • Advances in Geometry, Algebra, Trigonometry, Calculus
  • Binary mathematics and the notion of negative numbers
  • Infinite series for trigonometric functions (centuries before Europe)

The Classical / Golden Age (400–1600 CE)

This period produced the greatest cluster of Indian mathematical talent. Key figures include Aryabhata, Varahamihira, Brahmagupta, Bhaskara I, Mahavira, Bhaskara II, Madhava of Sangamagrama, and Nilakantha Somayaji.

02

The Concept of Zero

Zero was discovered by an anonymous Indian. Its development transformed mathematics and is one of India's most profound intellectual gifts to the world.

2500–1500 BCE
Indus Valley Civilization
Earliest recorded use of a decimal system; zero concept begins to evolve.
476–550 CE
Aryabhata
Used a symbol for zero and recognized its importance in calculations.
598–668 CE
Brahmagupta
Formally defined zero as a number; introduced rules for arithmetic with zero (addition, subtraction, etc.).
Middle Ages
Spread to the World
Via Islamic scholars (Al-Khwarizmi), the concept reached Europe and became the foundation of modern mathematics.
Why Zero Matters
  • Acts as a placeholder in the decimal system
  • Enables representation of numbers of varying magnitudes
  • Essential in algebra, calculus, and virtually all modern mathematics

The Concept of Pi (π)

Pi represents the ratio of the circumference of a circle to its diameter. Indian mathematicians contributed extensively to its approximation over many centuries.

π ≈ 22/7 ≈ 3.14159265359...
~800–200 BCE
Shulba Sutras
  • Ancient geometry texts containing early approximations of π
476–550 CE
Aryabhata
  • Calculated π ≈ 3.1416 in his work Aryabhatiya
  • Used a geometric method to estimate the value
7th Century CE
Bhaskara I
  • Used a polygon with 384 sides to estimate π more accurately
14th–16th Century
Madhava
  • Developed infinite series expansions for π and trigonometric functions
  • Predated European calculus developments by centuries
04

The Number System

India's development of the decimal numeral system is considered one of its greatest intellectual achievements. It is the most widely used number system in the world today.

~2500–1500 BCE
Indus Valley Civilization
Earliest evidence of numerical symbols for counting and recording quantities.
~3rd Century BCE
Brahmi Numerals
Among the earliest written representations of numerical values in India.
3rd BCE – 3rd CE
Kharosthi Numeral System
A positional decimal system with symbols for 1–9 and zero; found on inscriptions, coins, and artifacts.
4th–6th Century CE
Gupta Numeral System
Regional numerals used in the Gupta Empire; gradually replaced by the decimal system.
The Decimal System — Why It Won
  • Uses just 10 symbols (0–9) to represent all numbers
  • Place-value notation simplifies all arithmetic
  • Zero as both a placeholder and a number — an Indian invention
  • Transmitted to Europe via Islamic scholars (Al-Khwarizmi)
05

Pythagorean Theorem in India

The theorem states: in a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides. While named after the Greek mathematician Pythagoras, evidence shows this knowledge existed in India long before.

c² = a² + b²
800–200 BCE
Sulba Sutras
  • Geometry rules for constructing religious altars
  • Demonstrate understanding of right-angled triangle relationships
Ancient
Baudhayana & Apastamba
  • Geometric methods for constructing right angles
  • Diagonal calculations aligning with Pythagorean principles
476–550 CE
Aryabhata
  • Discussed relationships between chord lengths and circle radii — related to the theorem
Important Note
  • The theorem is named after Pythagoras because he is credited with the first formal proof in ancient Greece
  • Indian texts demonstrated the knowledge and application of the principle without a formal proof
06

Vedic Mathematics

A system of mathematical techniques attributed to Swami Bharati Krishna Tirtha, who published the book "Vedic Mathematics" in 1965, claiming the methods are derived from the Vedas.

Sutras
  • 16 Sutras (aphorisms) and 13 Upa-sutras (sub-sutras)
  • Claimed to cover addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, roots
Mental Calculation
  • Emphasis on fast mental computation
  • Claimed to be faster than traditional methods
Criticism
  • Legitimacy debated among mathematicians and scholars
  • Critics argue techniques are not significantly different from established methods
Educational Use
  • Introduced in some schools as a teaching tool
  • Can help develop mental math speed and accuracy

Spotlight: Kaprekar's Constant (6174)

How it works
  • Take any 4-digit number where not all digits are the same
  • Arrange digits in descending order, then ascending order
  • Subtract the smaller from the larger
  • Repeat — you will always reach 6174 within 7 steps
e.g. Start: 1234 → 4321 − 1234 = 3087
→ 8730 − 0378 = 8352 → 8532 − 2358 = 6174

Spotlight: Srinivasa Ramanujan

An equation for me has no meaning unless it represents a thought of God.

— Srinivasa Ramanujan (1887–1920)
Fast Facts
  • Born 22 December 1887 in Erode, Tamil Nadu
  • Wrote to British mathematician G.H. Hardy in January 1913
  • Brought to Cambridge University in 1916 by Hardy
  • Published more than 340 mathematical papers
  • Film based on his life: "The Man Who Knew Infinity"
  • 2012 declared National Mathematics Year by the Government of India

The Kerala School of Mathematics

Active between the 14th and 16th centuries CE in Kerala, this school made extraordinary advances that predated European developments in calculus and infinite series by several centuries.

Trigonometry
  • Infinite series for sine, cosine, tangent
  • Predated European calculus developments
Astronomy
  • Planetary motion models
  • Lunar and solar eclipse predictions
Key Texts
  • Yuktibhāṣā — Jyesthadeva
  • Tantrasangraha — Nilakantha Somayaji
  • Karana Paddhati — Sankara Variar
Calendar & Legacy
  • Calendar reforms via astronomical calculations
  • Built upon Aryabhata, Brahmagupta, and others

Quick Self-Test

Click each question to reveal the answer. Test yourself before checking!

Q1 Who formally defined zero as a number with its own mathematical properties? Reveal ▾
Brahmagupta (598–668 CE). He introduced rules for arithmetic operations with zero in his work Brahmasphutasiddhanta.
Q2 What value did Aryabhata calculate for Pi? Reveal ▾
Aryabhata approximated π ≈ 3.1416, a significant improvement over earlier estimates, in his work Aryabhatiya.
Q3 What is the Kaprekar Constant, and who discovered it? Reveal ▾
6174, discovered by D.R. Kaprekar in 1949. Any 4-digit number (not all same digits) will reach 6174 within 7 iterations of subtracting its ascending-digit form from its descending-digit form.
Q4 What was the Kerala School's most significant mathematical achievement? Reveal ▾
The discovery of infinite series expansions for trigonometric functions (sine, cosine, tangent), which predated European developments in calculus by several centuries.
Q5 What astronomical idea did Aryabhata propose that was centuries ahead of Europe? Reveal ▾
A heliocentric model of the solar system — he correctly stated that the Earth revolves around the Sun.
Q6 What is Mahaviracharya's most famous work and what does its title mean? Reveal ▾
"Ganita Sara Sangraha," meaning "Compendium of the Essence of Mathematics." It is a comprehensive treatise covering arithmetic, geometry, algebra, and number theory.
Q7 Who is credited with the first formal proof of the Pythagorean Theorem, and why not an Indian mathematician? Reveal ▾
Pythagoras of ancient Greece is credited with the first formal proof. While Indian texts (Sulba Sutras, Baudhayana) demonstrated knowledge and application of the principle, they did not present it as a formal mathematical proof.
Q8 How many mathematical papers did Ramanujan publish, and which university did he attend in England? Reveal ▾
Ramanujan published more than 340 papers. He was brought to Cambridge University in 1916 by G.H. Hardy.
Q9 What are the Sulba Sutras, and approximately when were they written? Reveal ▾
The Sulba Sutras are ancient Indian texts dealing with geometry for constructing religious altars. They date from approximately 800–200 BCE and contain some of the earliest known geometric knowledge, including Pythagorean-type relationships.
Q10 Name the four sections of Bhaskaracharya's Siddhanta Shiromani. Reveal ▾
1. Lilavati (arithmetic & geometry), 2. Bijaganita (algebra), 3. Grahaganita (mathematics of the planets), 4. Goladhyaya (spherical trigonometry).
